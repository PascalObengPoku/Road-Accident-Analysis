# Road Accident Analysis Report (2021–2022)

##  Overview

This project analyzes road accident data from **2021–2022**, uncovering key patterns in accident frequency, severity, and contributing factors. The goal is to generate **data-driven insights** that can inform policy, improve road safety, and reduce fatalities.

---

## Executive Summary

- **307,973** total accidents analyzed

- **563,302** vehicles involved

- **417,883** total casualties

- **85%** of accidents are *slight severity*

- Accidents are concentrated in **urban areas**, **daylight**, and **dry road conditions**

- Peak risk periods: **evenings**, **Fridays**, and **Oct–Nov**

---

## Geographic Insights

### High-Risk Districts

* Birmingham – 6,165
* Leeds – 4,140
* Manchester – 3,132
* Bradford – 3,006
* Westminster – 2,811

**Implication:**
Urban centers dominate accident occurrences due to **high traffic density and congestion**.

---

## Temporal Trends

### By Year

* **2021:** 163,554 accidents
* **2022:** 144,419 accidents

**Implication:**
A noticeable decline in 2022 suggests **improved policies or behavioral changes**.

---

### By Month (Top Peaks)

* November – 29,095
* October – 28,368
* July – 26,953

**Implication:**
Late-year peaks (Oct–Nov) may be driven by:

* Reduced daylight
* Weather changes
* Increased travel activity

---

### By Day of Week

* Friday – 68,294 (highest)
* Tuesday & Wednesday – consistently high
* Sunday – lowest

**Implication:**
**End-of-week fatigue + increased movement = higher accident risk**

---

## Time-Based Patterns

### Peak Hours

* 17:00 – 3,777
* 17:30 – 3,616
* 15:30 – 3,578

**Implication:**
Accidents peak during **rush hours (3 PM – 6 PM)**.

### High-Casualty Events

* Example: *Dec 22, 2021 at 22:10 → 48 casualties*

Common during:

* Evenings
* Holiday periods

---

## Vehicle Involvement

* Cars – 325,922 (dominant)
* Vans – 21,298
* Motorcycles – significant share

**Implication:**

* Cars contribute most due to volume
* Motorcycles show **higher relative risk exposure**

---

## Environmental Conditions

### Weather

* Fine weather – 330,311
* Rain – 49,082

**Implication:**
Most accidents occur in good weather due to:

* Higher traffic volume
* Driver overconfidence

---

### Road Surface

* Dry – 279,445
* Wet/Damp – 114,697

**Implication:**
Wet roads significantly **increase severity**, even if less frequent.

---

### Light Conditions

* Daylight – 304,963
* Darkness (lit) – 82,167

**Implication:**
Higher daylight accidents reflect **activity levels**, not necessarily higher danger.

---

## Area Type

* Urban – 255,864
* Rural – 162,019

**Implication:**
Urban areas dominate due to:

* Intersections
* Traffic congestion

---

## Speed Analysis

* 30 mph – 255,418 (highest)
* 60 mph – 71,402
* 70 mph – 35,034

💡 **Implication:**
Most accidents occur in **low-speed zones**, reinforcing **urban driving risks**.

---

## Accident Severity

* Slight – 351,436 (~85%)
* Serious – 59,312
* Fatal – 7,135

**Implication:**
While most accidents are minor, **fatalities remain significant and preventable**.

---

## Key Risk Patterns

### High-Risk Combination

**Urban + 30 mph + Car + Daylight + Weekday Evening**

### Critical Risk Factors

* Traffic congestion
* Rush hour driving
* Seasonal/weather changes
* Human behavior (fatigue, distraction)

---

## Recommendations

### Policy & Infrastructure

* Improve traffic control in urban hotspots
* Enhance road lighting
* Deploy smart traffic systems

### Driver Safety

* Awareness campaigns for:

  * Rush hour driving
  * Wet road conditions
* Enforce speed compliance in 30 mph zones

### Seasonal Measures

* Increase enforcement during:

  * October–November
  * Holiday periods

### Targeted Interventions

* Motorcycle safety programs
* Urban congestion management

## Conclusion

This analysis reveals that road accidents are less about extreme conditions and more about everyday driving exposure and human behavior. The highest risks emerge not in unusual scenarios, but in routine environments—urban roads, moderate speeds, and peak commuting hours.

While the majority of accidents are slight, the consistent presence of serious and fatal incidents highlights a critical gap in preventive measures. Patterns across time, location, and conditions point to a clear opportunity: targeted, data-driven interventions can significantly reduce both frequency and severity.

By focusing on urban congestion, rush-hour dynamics, seasonal spikes, and driver behavior, stakeholders can move from reactive responses to proactive safety strategies.

Ultimately, this project demonstrates how data can be leveraged not just to understand past incidents, but to inform smarter policies, improve infrastructure, and save lives at scale.
